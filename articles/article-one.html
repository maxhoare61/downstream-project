The term ‚Äòcarbon footprint‚Äô refers to emissions that are created as a product of our individual actions. Over the last two decades, this idea has become a centrepiece of debates about climate change. Why do we often frame this enormously complex problem, whose causes are entrenched in all areas of society, in terms of the simple decisions we make each day?

Tracing Roots

In 2004, BP‚Äôs marketing underwent a remarkable transformation. The oil behemoth ditched its dated BP-crest logo for a green flower, adopted the tagline ‚ÄúBeyond Petroleum‚Äù, and featured prominent adverts in major newspapers which prompted readers to ‚ÄúFind out the size of your household‚Äôs carbon footprint, learn how you can reduce it, and see how we‚Äôre reducing ours at bp.com‚Äù.

At the heart of this overhaul was BP‚Äôs ‚Äúcarbon footprint calculator‚Äù. The concept was simple: people could go online, enter details about their daily activities into the calculator (driving, electricity use, flights, etc.), and it calculates their personal annual carbon emissions for them.

This idea, framing climate change in terms of one‚Äôs personal actions, became a key part of the narrative used by fossil fuel companies to distract from the real causes of emissions ‚Äì their operations. BP and other major emitters sought to portray themselves as part of the climate solution, while subtly shifting the locus of responsibility to individual consumers.

Big Oil's New Century Makeover

By the early 2000s, public consensus around the reality of climate change was solidifying, which meant two things. Firstly, as one of the world‚Äôs largest oil companies, BP faced what seemed to be an existential threat in the form of climate action. Secondly, the average person now had to deal with climate anxiety; the stress of an uncertain future, courtesy of a warming climate.

BP saw this and set out to portray itself as a brand-new company, with a new, green image. The carbon footprint, an emblem of holding the public responsible for private companies‚Äô actions, was to sit at the heart of this new identity. By encouraging individuals to measure their own emissions, BP‚Äôs messaging implied that climate change is the result of personal choices. Through adverts and rebranding they silently pushed the message ‚ÄúThat‚Äôs right, I‚Äôm doing my part. Are you?‚Äù. Climate scientists have observed that this move was part of a broader shift in tactics by fossil fuel interests from outright climate science denial to a technique called ‚Äúdeflection‚Äù. Deflection is when an actor is seeking to challenge a narrative in public discourse, and instead of outright denying it, seems to agree, while serving up a slightly altered version of events to an unassuming audience. BP‚Äôs massive PR campaign not only places the burden of responsibility on consumers but alleviates corporate polluters. Instead of being seen as one of the worst perpetrators of environmental havoc, now they were trying to make a difference. This positioned the company as doing their part by providing tools and encouragement, despite their core business still being overwhelmingly in high emissions activities.

On the other hand, the notion of the carbon footprint provided a sense of control for everyday people coping with the reality of climate change. The weight of knowing that human activities are destroying the planet is crushing and hard to digest. Calculating your carbon footprint was given to the public as a small thing that they could do to help fight the climate catastrophe and the associated anxiety. By allowing people to quantify their personal contributions to climate change, they could aim to make improvements and measure the results. Even if there was little that most people can do to stave off the destruction of the environment, it can be helpful to feel you are minimising your personal impact.

What Does the Data Say?
Personal Vs. Corporate Emissions

If everyone is responsible for their carbon footprint, how big are those footprints really? How do they compare to the carbon pollution emitted by large fossil fuel companies? The data overwhelmingly show the emissions output of individual‚Äôs, even in high-income countries, are a fraction of a fraction of corporate carbon footprints. In Australia ‚Äì which has one of the highest per-capita carbon footprints ‚Äì the average person‚Äôs activities produce about 15.4 tons of CO‚ÇÇ per year. Multiplying that by almost 27 million Australians yields 415.8 million tons ‚Äì which is significant. However, that is a meagre 5.6% of the 7.4 billion tonnes of Co2 produced by just 5 entities (Saudi Aramco, Coal India, CHN Energy, National Iranian Oil Co., and Jinneng Group) in 2023. In other words, if everyone in Australia used no electricity, gas or petrol, it would take 17 years to recuperate the emissions produced by those 5 companies in a single year. This underscores that climate change is not simply the result of billions of people negligently leaving lights on; rather, it‚Äôs driven by industrial activity and energy production choices. As experts have pointed out, the real levers with the power to make change -systemic factors like how electricity is generated, how goods are produced, and what policies govern energy use determine the bulk of emissions ‚Äì are influenced by corporate and governmental control far more than any average person.

Different Types of Emissions

To understand why society's focus on individual carbon footprints is so flawed, it helps to know about the concepts of 'scope' emissions. Scope 1, scope 2 and scope 3 emissions are three categories that have become the accepted method for distinguishing the different types of emissions produced by an entity. The basic premise is that by categorising the link between a set of emissions and a company‚Äôs activities, we have some idea about how responsible the company is for them. Thus, this system has found use in assisting governments when categorizing emissions for policy purposes and for corporations pursuing decarbonization initiatives.

Scope 1 emissions refer to direct emissions created by a company's assets being used for its purposes. For example, in a mining company, these would include emissions from running large trucks to transport rubble or from burning diesel to power heavy machinery for excavation.

Scope 2 emissions are those produced indirectly through the purchase of electricity. For instance, a large corporation creates Scope 2 emissions by using electricity in its head offices.

Scope 3 emissions are those that would not occur without the company's activities but are produced by assets or sources that the company does not directly own or control. Examples include emissions from running a Camry for Toyota or emissions caused by the transportation and distribution of goods before they arrive on the shelves at Coles.

To use an analogy, we can think of the emissions created in different stages of the lifecycle of a product as water flowing down a river.

Bonus: Lessons from Covid-19

It is telling that even when individual behaviour changed dramatically during the COVID-19 pandemic the drop in global CO‚ÇÇ emissions was not significant ‚Äì between 6-8% in 2020. The chart below shows the quarterly national emissions data for Australia, with the shaded area approximately covering the time affected by the pandemic. While there is a definitive slump in emissions evident during the pandemic, it is hardly on the order implied by the mantra that our ‚Äúindividual efforts make all the difference‚Äù. Moreover, the decrease in emissions during the pandemic represented part of a broader downward trend due to the decarbonisation of the grid, evident in the gradual shrinking of the blue ‚Äòelectricity‚Äô layer over time. All in all, the emissions impact of this society wide change in behaviour was nominal.

This is a result of the fact that most of the emissions caused on an individual level are indirect, scope 2 or scope 3 emissions. If you have taken a survey for a carbon footprint calculator, you know that a big part of the calculation involves the amount of flights you take, and how much power you use at home. These are examples of indirect sources of emissions, since they are not directly produced by your actions or property you own. What the carbon footprint doesn‚Äôt account for is that, buy-and-large, the amount of emissions output by these systems and products does not or cannot respond to changes in your personal demand. This means that even if you abstain from taking a flight, or turning on a light, the emissions that you are ‚Äòavoiding‚Äô by abstaining from the activity have already been produced or will be regardless. Additionally, some activities such as air travel are essential for modern life, and seldom have sustainable alternatives. This means, if you want to reduce your footprint, you need to make sacrifices and miss out on opportunities.

The large role of indirect emissions in our personal carbon footprint creates a barrier to how much a person‚Äôs emissions can be decreased through personal action alone. Because the amount of indirect emissions we produce is determined by large systems such as the grid, the emissions intensity of the services they provide can increase or decrease independently of our actions. For instance, say you stop driving to work or taking flights to reduce your emissions. Now instead of producing emissions from car or plane exhaust fumes, you will be at home, using a larger amount of electricity to cook or keep the lights on. While there are some efforts we can make to improve the energy efficiency of our home, the emissions intensity of the electricity we use to power appliances, charge our phone and watch TV cannot be altered. This is determined by the generation mix in the grid. It should be noted that the point here is not to show that we cannot make change or set a positive example for others by choosing to make sustainable decisions. It is that the expectation that our personal decisions alone can make a tangible impact in fighting climate change is deeply misguided. This view neglects to acknowledge that we live in a large and interconnected society where the emissions output of most everyday activities is dictated by large organisations and grids that we do not necessarily have control over.

This is further shown through the drastic improvements in the sustainability of Australia‚Äôs grid energy. Between 2000 and 2023, the amount of CO‚ÇÇ produced per unit of energy produced in Australia has decreased by 47%. This enormous improvement, which affects the carbon footprint of all Australians, had nothing to do with individual decision-making. Rather, it was brought about through placing pressure on those in power, targeted policy, changing cultural attitudes and coordinated efforts to add renewable generation to the grid.

On the other hand, there are certain sets of emissions we do have control over. Typically these are Scope 1 emissions‚Äîthe direct emissions from sources you own or control. This includes things like the fuel you burn in your personal vehicle, and emissions from gas heaters in your home, or any other combustion that happens under your direct ownership. These are the easiest to influence directly, since they are directly produced through your actions. Therefore, transitioning to electric vehicles, improving home insulation, or switching from gas to electric cooking can significantly reduce this category. Albeit, these changes do require an initial investment, so they may not always be an option. Other changes such as using public transport, or riding a bike to work can be both affordable and effective at reducing your personal impact, and can give some degree of satisfaction that you are contributing to limiting human impact on the environment.

Influence on Public Perception and Discourse

BP‚Äôs carbon footprint campaign and similar industry efforts have had a discernible impact on public perceptions of climate responsibility. The very fact that ‚Äúcarbon footprint‚Äù entered everyday vocabulary in the 2000s is testament to the campaign‚Äôs reach. Before BP‚Äôs push, the term was virtually non-existent in public discourse; afterwards, it became a centrepiece of climate conversations, educational programs, and even government advisories. Google search trends for ‚Äúcarbon footprint‚Äù spike after 2004, reflecting how quickly the concept caught on. Many people began meticulously tracking their personal carbon impacts ‚Äì from switching light bulbs to calculating flight emissions ‚Äì convinced that individual action was the paramount solution to climate change. Climate educators have noted how this focus can crowd out discussion of systemic action. Naomi Oreskes, the Harvard science historian, offers a telling anecdote: when giving public talks on climate change, she is almost invariably asked ‚ÄúWhat can I do, personally, to fix this problem?‚Äù far more often than she is asked about holding companies or governments accountable. This reflects a success of the fossil-fuel PR narrative: the public internalized the idea that personal lifestyle changes are the key to addressing climate change, while discussion of corporate responsibility or political action was comparatively muted. Indeed, Oreskes attributes that common sentiment in part to the marketing campaigns by BP, Exxon and others that relentlessly promoted personal carbon footprints.

However, there are signs that society‚Äôs view of an individual-based approach to fighting climate change is increasingly dim. Social scientists and climate communicators have increasingly critiqued the over-emphasis on individual action as a form of ‚Äúdiscursive manipulation‚Äù that can delay collective solutions. On social media, posts such as the one featured at the start of the article reflect the cultural shift. When a global company‚Äôs tweet encouraging climate action gets ratio‚Äôd by a no name account by a factor of 136, it is a sign that people no longer see empty climate platitudes from corporations through the same rose-tinted lens that they once did.

Environmental organizations now often stress balancing personal steps with systemic change ‚Äì essentially re-educating the public that one cannot simply shop or recycle their way out of the climate crisis if policy and corporate practices remain unchanged. Importantly, public opinion is evolving: surveys indicate that people are increasingly aware of corporate culpability for climate change. A national survey in the United States (Yale, 2019) found that 57% of Americans believe fossil fuel companies have either ‚Äúa great deal‚Äù or ‚Äúa moderate amount‚Äù of responsibility for the damages caused by global warming. A similar majority supported requiring those companies to pay for a portion of climate damage costs. This suggests a shift in perception ‚Äì despite years of ‚Äúcarbon footprint‚Äù messaging, more than half the public now explicitly holds the industry accountable in substantial measure. In essence, while BP‚Äôs campaign succeeded in injecting personal carbon footprints into the climate zeitgeist, it has not completely forestalled a growing public recognition that the climate crisis is, at its core, a systemic problem requiring systemic solutions.

Putting Emissions
Back in Perspective

Climate scientists like Michael Mann argue that while individual actions are helpful, we must not allow the narrative of personal responsibility to become a distraction or moral smoke-screen that delays larger interventions. Mann‚Äôs work The New Climate War calls out tactics like BP‚Äôs for what they are ‚Äì ‚Äúsoft denial‚Äù or deflection ‚Äì and emphasizes collective action: voting for climate-conscious leadership, enacting carbon pricing or emissions caps on industry, and supporting systemic changes in energy, transportation, and agriculture. We should aim for a more balanced climate discourse that encourages individual ‚Äúcarbon footprint‚Äù reductions (such as saving energy, diet shifts, and conscious consumption) firmly contextualized in a broader push that must also include major corporate and governmental emissions cuts. Rather than feeling personal guilt as the primary response to climate change, individuals should channel that concern into collective action and demand accountability from the heavy emitters.

To help explore this idea, Downstream project are building a 'reverse carbon calculator', a calculator aimed at showing our own emissions on the scale of large emitters, to help crystallise the large role of these actors in climate change.
