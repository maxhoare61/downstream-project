In the 2000s, the carbon footprint suddenly ascended to stardom in climate positive circles. The key message of this new dialogue was clear: We all need to do our part to stop climate change. But while average joes began cutting out meat and cycling to work, few stopped to question why major polluters were the biggest advocates of this movement.

Introduction

The popularity of the personal carbon footprint was borne out of the mistaken view that by creating emissions we are inherently complicit in global warming. This article contends that producing emissions is not an inherent moral failure, but rather a product of the entrenched supply chains we rely upon to carry out the energy transition. Corporations in the 2000s exploited this paradigm to turn the blame for climate change back onto consumers and distract from their own emissions.

BP, a Pioneer of Corporate Greenwashing

In 2000, petroleum supermajor BP completely transformed their brand. BP no longer stood for British Petroleum, they insisted, it meant Beyond Petroleum. As climate change began to loom large in the public conscious, people expected oil companies like BP to take action on climate change. Over the next five years BP would stake $200 million on a gamble to promote this green image, with hopes they could prolonge their inevitable reckoning in the form of the climate movement.

This marked a turning point for those opposing climate action. Unlike their competitors, who remained firmly in the camp of climate denial, BP cashed in on social currency by posturing as a climate friendly business. As a first mover in the oil industry, there were many firsts to be had, which strengthened the public credibility of their green appeal. BP was already the first oil company to state climate change exists, and the world‚Äôs largest solar module manufacturer . Now they had the slogan and advertising to match.

BPs Beyond Petroleum campaign was a renowned success. During the campaign, awareness of BPs brand in the US rose from below 10 percent to more than 60 percent, along with their new identity. Consumers even rated BP as the most environmentally friendly oil company in surveys .

Contrary to their Frutiger Aero aesthetic, the reality of BPs operations was almost inescapable as its marketing. Throughout the 2000s, media coverage was dominated by revelations of cost-cutting negligence‚Äîleading to massive pipeline leaks, refinery explosions, and ultimately the Deepwater Horizon disaster in 2010, the largest offshore oil spill in U.S. history. Outside of their catastrophic environmental impact, these disasters also resulted in over 26 casualties. It was clear BP thought they could market their way out of scrutiny, without addressing systematic issues in their underlying business.

As part of their campaign, BPs ‚Äúcarbon footprint calculator‚Äù served not to empower users, but to reinforce their charade of corporate environmental responsibility. It invited users to input lifestyle data, calculate their annual emissions, and commit to personal reductions. Whilst the concept of the carbon footprint calculator was not new, BP had co-opted it with the goal of exploiting consumers climate guilt‚Äîemphasising individual responsibility while shielding their own operations from scrutiny. By spotlighting consumer choices, BP redirected attention from the systemic emissions embedded in its business model. Putting Emissions Back Into Perspective

For decades now the numbers have been unambiguous: corporate polluters emit greenhouse gasses on a scale that dwarfs individual contributions. However, it can be hard to wrap your head the way emissions are described by academics and the media. What does a tonne of CO2 look like, really? How about a million tonnes? It is well known that people are very bad at comprehending numbers on this scale, especially when those numbers involve invisible gasses.

A better way is representing emissions is using units that we understand. One carbon footprint calculator tactfully uses the scale of ‚Äúearths‚Äù to represent individual emissions, showing how many multiples of the available resources on our planet would be needed if everyone on earth had your lifestyle. This tactic is highly effective in promoting understanding ‚Äì we immediately get the sense of whether our lifestyle choices are sustainable. Taking a page out of this book, we will explain the emissions of large corporate emissions relative to those of the average individual.

AGL, for instance, produces approximately one Australian‚Äôs annual emissions worth of CO2 of every 13 seconds. Through showing low, medium and high cases for personal emissions we aim to visualise the size of the impact we can have through personal lifestyle choices.
